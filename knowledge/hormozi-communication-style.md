# How to Communicate Like Alex Hormozi (Sales Reference)

Getting positive responses from cold emails means the initial hook is working. Now the job is to transition from "intriguing stranger" to "authoritative expert."

Alex Hormozi's sales philosophy (from *$100M Offers*, *$100M Leads*, and Acquisition.com training) revolves around **The Grand Slam Offer**: making an offer so good people feel stupid saying no. It emphasizes maintaining frame, selling the *next step* (not the whole service at once), and anchoring price to immense value.

---

## Phase 1: From Email Reply to the Call

When a prospect replies to a cold email, the only goal is to **sell the call, not the AI software**. Explaining features or pitching prices via email loses control and looks desperate.

### How to Communicate (Tone)
- **Be a Peer, Not a Beggar:** Act like a busy doctor diagnosing a patient — evaluating *them* for fit.
- **The "Takeaway":** Introduce slight friction to show you aren't desperate.
- **Keep it Short:** Long emails scream desperation.

### What to Say in the Reply
When they say "Tell me more" or "I'm interested," respond with a low-friction call to action:
> "Glad to hear it, [Name]. Honestly, I'm not sure if our AI qualification system is a fit for your specific [HVAC/Solar] setup yet. Do you have 10 minutes on Tuesday or Wednesday for a quick chat to see if it even makes sense to keep talking?"

### Handling "Just Send Me the Price" via Email
Never give the price via cold email — without value context, price is just an expense. Value-anchoring pivot:
> "Pricing depends entirely on your current lead volume and how many systems we need to integrate with. However, we operate on a strictly ROI-positive basis (e.g., if we don't save your reps 20 hours a week and book 10 extra installs, you don't pay). Are you open to a quick 10-min call to see if we can actually deliver that for you?"

## Phase 2: The Call & Communicating Price

Closing happens on the phone/Zoom, not in an inbox.

### Call Structure ("The Closer" Framework)
- **Clarify:** "Why did you take this call today?"
- **Label:** Identify their current pain (e.g., "So your guys are wasting hours driving to unqualified solar leads?").
- **Overview:** Pitch the Dream Outcome, not the mechanism. Not "LLMs, Python, API integrations" — instead: "A system that answers every customer text in 3 seconds, pre-qualifies their roof for solar, and books the appointment on your calendar automatically."
- **Sell the Vacation, Not the Flight:** They care about closed deals and saved time, not how the AI works.

### When and How to Say the Price
Say it at the very end, after stacking value and stating the guarantee:
1. **Stack the Value:** "You get the 24/7 AI secretary, the calendar integration, and the SMS follow-up sequence."
2. **The Guarantee:** "If we don't generate at least X qualified appointments in your first 30 days, we work for free until we do."
3. **The Drop:** "The investment to get this set up and running is $X."
4. **The Golden Rule: SHUT UP.** Do not say a word after dropping the price. Let the silence hang.

---

## Phase 3: The Proposal & Closing Strategy

### The "Anti-Proposal" Rule
Avoid the standard "send a proposal and wait" routine — momentum dies and leverage is lost when they're left to "think about it."

### If a Proposal Is Unavoidable
- **Do it Live:** Walk through the proposal together on the call.
- **Collect Commitment on the Call.**
- **If forced to send async, never send without booking the follow-up call first:**
  - Wrong: "Here is the proposal, let me know what you think."
  - Right: "I will draft this up today. Let's put 10 minutes on the calendar for Thursday at 2 PM to walk through it together so I can answer any technical questions."

## Phase 4: The Follow-Up Strategy

If they ghost after a proposal or call: **"follow up until they buy or die,"** done elegantly. Most sales happen between the 5th and 12th contact. Space follow-ups out over time (Day 2, 4, 7, 14, 30, then monthly).

### Follow-Up Cadence Types
1. **Value-Add Email (show, don't tell)** — send proof, not "just checking in":
   > "Hey [Name], we just rolled out a similar AI qualification tool for an HVAC guy in Texas. It disqualified 40 tire-kickers last week and booked 12 solid high-ticket replacement jobs. Thought of you. Let me know when you want to get yours up and running."
2. **Scarcity/Urgency Email** — real scarcity only:
   > "Hey [Name], we are onboarding 3 new solar installers next week and our tech team is capping bandwidth for the month to ensure quality. If you want to get the AI secretary live before the summer rush, we need to finalize by Friday."
3. **The 9-Word Email** — extremely short, yes/no only:
   > Subject: [Company Name] AI — "Are you still looking to automate your lead qualification this month?"
4. **The Takeaway/Break-Up Email** — pull the offer away after weeks of silence:
   > "Hey [Name], I haven't heard back from you, so I'm assuming getting these leads automated isn't a priority right now. I'm going to close out your file so we can focus on our current clients. If things change, feel free to reach out."

### Campaign Checklist
- Goal of cold reply: book the 10-min triage/discovery call.
- Goal of triage call: book the closing call (or pitch immediately if ready).
- Pitch the outcome (booked jobs), not the vehicle (AI).
- State price confidently at the end of the call, followed by total silence.
- Review proposals live on the call.
- Follow-ups mix case studies, urgency, and direct questions — never apologize for following up.

## Coaching vs. Teaching

In complex B2B deals, act as a business consultant, not a feature-explainer.
- **The Teaching Trap:** Prospects rarely act on advice before they've financially committed. Only ~10% benefit from tactics; 100% benefit from having limiting beliefs broken.
- **Coaching:** Use a question-based framework to lead the prospect to their own conclusions.

## Three Essential Types of Sales Statements
1. **Questions** — the primary tool for surfacing struggles, goals, and why prior attempts failed without triggering defensiveness.
2. **Restatements** — repeat the core message back: "So what I'm hearing is this is the primary issue you've been trying to solve... does that sound about right?"
3. **Short Stories and Analogies** — illustrate outcomes instead of explaining mechanisms (e.g., the fishing analogy, the weight-loss analogy).

## The 3A Reframing Framework
Used after a prospect says anything other than "yes":
- **Acknowledge** — repeat what they said, build rapport, buy time.
- **Associate** — tie their hesitation to the behavior of successful/rational clients ("That's an amazing question — it shows you're a rational person making a serious decision").
- **Ask (the attack)** — ask a question that probes the premise of the objection ("Which certifications are you looking for specifically?").

## Five Rules of Ethical Reframing
1. **The person asking questions is in control.** Never ask "Do you have any questions?" — it invites objections and cedes control.
2. **You cannot catch smoke.** Never win an argument and lose a sale; ask for the specific concern behind vague hesitation ("I need to think about it").
3. **Tell them what their question means** — use expertise to define their intent (e.g., reframe "I already have an advisor" as evidence they're past the learning curve).
4. **Use "straw men" for tough truths** instead of confronting directly — the "earlier today" foil, the successful-customer foil, the authority appeal.
5. **Retain childlike curiosity** — seek to understand, not to beat the prospect; a simple "Huh?" buys time.

## Psychology of "No"
"Yes" can feel vulnerable; "No" feels safe and autonomous.
- **No-based questions** let the prospect agree while feeling protected: "Would you be opposed to moving forward today?" / "Would it be completely unreasonable for us to get started?"
- The **"Fair Enough" close**: "What if we did X instead, fair enough?"

## Objection Scripts
- **Spouse objection:** Acknowledge it's sensible, then ask: "What parts do you think they wouldn't like?"
- **Timing objection:** Acknowledge, associate with success stories who felt the same, then ask: "What would make it a good time?"
- **Direct confrontation** (late-call hesitation): "What are you afraid of?"

## Final Mindset
The goal is to help qualified prospects make a decision that actually helps them. If the product is genuinely the solution, persistent, well-paced follow-up is a service, not an annoyance — sales is a "dance" based on consent, never a fight.
